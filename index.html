Shadows Don’t Lie and Lines Can’t Bend! Generative Models don’t know Projective Geometry...for now

Ayush Sarkar1*, Asher Mai1*, Amitabh Mahapatra1*, Svetlana Lazebnik1, David A. Forsyth1, Anand Bhattad2
1University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign 2Toyota Technological Institute at Chicago
* Equally contributing first author
Paper arXiv Video Code Data

AI-generated images, while visually appealing, often have errors in shadow alignment and vanishing point accuracy. Our model effectively detects these systematic inconsistencies, aiding in the identification of such images.

Abstract

Generative models can produce impressively realistic images. This paper demonstrates that generated images have geometric features different from those of real images. We build a set of collections of generated images, prequalified to fool simple, signal-based classifiers into believing they are real. We then show that prequalified generated images can be identified reliably by classifiers that only look at geometric properties. We use three such classifiers. All three classifiers are denied access to image pixels, and look only at derived geometric features. The first classifier looks at the perspective field of the image, the second looks at lines detected in the image, and the third looks at relations between detected objects and shadows. Our procedure detects generated images more reliably than SOTA local signal based detectors, for images from a number of distinct generators. Saliency maps suggest that the classifiers can identify geometric problems reliably. We conclude that current generators cannot reliably reproduce geometric properties of real images.

Results

ROC Curves Assessing Projective Geometry Cues in AI-Generated images by Stable Diffusion XL. We train distinct models for Indoor Scenes in the top row, Outdoor scenes in the middle row, and Indoor + Outdoor Scenes in the last row. (a): Across the Full Test Set, our Perspective Field classifier and our Line Segment classifier are somewhat more accurate (AUCs of 0.94 and 0.98) than the Prequalifier (AUC of 0.93). Prequalification allows us to check whether these methods might be using signal cues. They are not, as (b) and (c) demonstrate. (b): Results for the Unconfident Test Set (where the Prequalifier has an AUC of 0.47, as it should); note how our classifiers can still reliably identify generated images. (c): Results for the Misclassified Test Set (where the Prequalifier has an AUC of 0.00, as it should); note how our classifiers remain reliable. We conclude that generated indoor images contain geometric structures not seen in real images, and that these structures very reliably identify generated images.
